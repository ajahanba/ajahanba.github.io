I am a researcher, educator and an advocate for ethics and diversity in computing. My general area of research is in Ubiquitous and Social Computing, where I am interested in developing mathematical and computational models that leverage the proliferation of data and breakthroughs in machine learning to design human-centric systems that accommodate for human needs, capabilities and behaviors. Specifically my research focus is on sensing, modeling, understanding and predicting human behavior using the “digital traces” that are generated daily in our online and offline lives. In particular my research has focused on viewing “digital traces” in two ways (1) firstly as distinctive realms in which much of human experience now resides. That is studying socio-technical systems and modeling social dynamics of human behavior in these platforms. (2) to view digital data platforms as generalizable microcosms of society and thus develop mathematical and computational models to better understand societies and social phenomenas at different spatial scales. By analyzing the use of technology at these different scales, from the micro level, such as person-to-person or person-to-system interactions, to the macro level, such as population dynamics, I aim to build systems based on better models of human behavior that can help us construct more powerful human centric services. I am an affiliate assistant professor of Sociology at University of Washington, where I teach a course on how data and technology is impacting society and discussing ethics and biases in data science. >

Since 2018 I have joined UN Global Pulse team, the flagship innovation team of the United Nations Secretary-General on big data, where I apply my expertise on ethics and applied machine learning to help shape policies that directly target SDG. I also hold an honorary research position at University College London where I previously obtained my PhD and worked as a Post-Doc. I am an affiliate of the eScience Institute at University of Washington.
