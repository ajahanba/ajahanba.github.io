My research focuses on ubiquitous computing where I leverage human behavioural patterns to design human-centric systems; and Human Computer Interactions (HCI) where I aim to uncover the perception of users’ interaction with such systems.



Human-centric systems are systems that are designed to accommodate human needs, capabilities and behaviours. They breach the disconnected gap between the designers of a system and its end-user, by studying and adding information regarding human behaviour to the design process of the product or service. By granting this philosophy, the human-centric systems result in improving the experience of the end-users’s interaction with the system. In order to learn the human traits that have an impact  on the design of these systems, social scientist had been relying on observing human subjects.  However, in the past decade, thanks to the market penetration of smartphone, an unprecedented amount of data about human behaviour has surfaced, enabling scientist to learn more about human behaviour. Take human mobility as an example, research has shown that each individual being can be characterised by a significant probability to return to a few highly frequently visited locations. Similar predictable behaviours are observed in our daily routines and activities too. By understanding these behavioural patterns, we can design human-centric systems that are better catered for the end-user and are able to address problems that traditional design approaches failed to do. 


For example learning patterns of users engagement and interactions with their smartphone could help us deliver services such as Notification Messages more efficiently, that is such that less energy is consumed on the device; as well as more effectively (less interruption to the user). Moreover, these human behavioural patterns are not only enablers for providing better services to the end-users, but also could be leveraged to infer more knowledge about the city and the society. 

A key vehicle that facilitates this research stream and enables  mining and leveraging of these behavioural patterns, is data collection.  As the result of this dependency, a research challenge that arises is on how to collect data from people during their daily life.  A viable way of data collection is crowdsourcing. Crowdsourcing is a new paradigm which includes a broad range of applications: from participatory sensing, where individuals contribute by implicitly collecting sensory data (such as noise and pollution), to a more explicit engagement of the citizens (such as surveys and opinions). However, the data generated through a crowdsourcing method is non-uniformly distributed in geographical and temporal spaces. In some locations, we may have much more data than what we really need. The research challenge that arises is how to perform down-sampling method (e.g., compressive sensing) so to reduce a system’s load. The research challenge in here is that given a limited budget, how to configure the sampling strategy so to adaptively decide on the granularity of the sampled locations and time periods such that the quality of the received data (e.g., the coverage or accuracy) for a specific application is maximised.



Finally, as we design for these human-centred technologies, we are continuously collecting data and inferring contextual information about the users. Thus one inevitable challenge that arises is regarding the privacy of the users and their consent over the captured data. Therefore, it is important to understand possible privacy threats that the users may incur when interacting with these technologies.
